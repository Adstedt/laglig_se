# Arbetsmiljö för tjänsteföretag -- Laglistaanalys

## 1. Overview

This list is a purpose-built subset of the full 112-document Arbetsmiljö reference list, narrowed to 55 documents across 7 sections. It targets service-sector organizations -- consulting firms, IT companies, staffing agencies, and other office-based employers -- that do not operate manufacturing floors, construction sites, or heavy physical workplaces.

The subset is created by removing two parent sections entirely:

| Parent Section | Parent Name                   | Kept in Subset?              | Rationale                                                                                                                                                           |
| -------------- | ----------------------------- | ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 01             | ALLMANNA REGLER               | Yes (as section 01, 6 docs)  | Core framework laws apply to all employers regardless of industry                                                                                                   |
| 02             | HR                            | Yes (as section 02, 21 docs) | Employment law, data protection, leave rights -- universal for all employers                                                                                        |
| 03             | ARBETETS UTFORMNING           | Yes (as section 03, 10 docs) | Workplace design rules include office ergonomics, lone work, and psychosocial provisions relevant to service firms                                                  |
| 04             | FYSISK ARBETSMILJO            | **Removed**                  | Covers physical hazards such as noise, vibrations, lifting, and heavy machinery -- not applicable to office/service settings                                        |
| 05             | ARBETSUTRUSTNING              | Yes (as section 04, 5 docs)  | Reduced from parent's 8 docs; only display-screen and ladder/stepladder provisions retained                                                                         |
| 06             | BRAND- OCH EXPLOSIONSSAKERHET | Yes (as section 06, 6 docs)  | Fire and explosion safety still applies to any employer operating from physical premises                                                                            |
| 07             | ELSAKERHET                    | Yes (as section 07, 3 docs)  | Electrical safety applies to all building occupiers                                                                                                                 |
| 08             | KEMISK ARBETSMILJO            | Yes (as section 05, 4 docs)  | Chemical safety scoped to REACH/CLP and product labelling obligations; physical handling provisions dropped                                                         |
| 09             | OVRIGA DOKUMENT               | **Removed**                  | The parent's 43-document catch-all section covers specialized topics (radiation, Seveso, asbestos, construction waste, etc.) outside the scope of service companies |

The parent lost 57 documents (7 from section 04, 3 from section 05 reduction, and 43 from section 09, plus the 7th doc from section 01 -- AFS 2023:15 on medical controls -- which was also dropped because mandatory health surveillance is tied to physical exposure risks). The remaining 55 documents form a coherent, auditable compliance checklist for knowledge-economy employers.

**laglig.se section notes:** This section anchors the entire compliance structure. The Arbetsmiljölag is the framework statute; the Arbetsmiljöförordning supplies implementation rules. AFS 2023:1 (SAM) is the mandatory management-system regulation that requires policy documents, risk assessments, incident investigation, and annual review. AFS 2023:2 kap. 2 (OSA) targets psychosocial risks -- stress, workload, harassment -- which are often the primary occupational health risks in service companies. First-aid and crisis-support provisions round out emergency preparedness duties. The tobacco law enforces smoke-free premises.

**Key observations:**

- Arbetsmiljöverket provisions (AFS) dominate the technical requirements, accounting for more than a third of all documents. Since AFS 2023, many older standalone provisions were consolidated into umbrella documents (AFS 2023:2, AFS 2023:10, AFS 2023:11, AFS 2023:12), each containing multiple chapter-level provisions that appear as separate entries in this list.
- Swedish statutes (SFS) provide the legal backbone -- framework acts, employment law, and sector-specific statutes.
- EU regulations are limited to GDPR, REACH, and CLP -- the three EU instruments with direct workplace applicability for service companies.
- MSB and fire-safety provisions are lightly represented because the service-company context excludes Seveso, heavy transport, and explosive-goods handling.

## 4. Content Pattern Assessment

**Regulatory structure pattern:** The list follows a clear hierarchy: framework acts (Arbetsmiljölagen, Arbetsmiljöförordningen) at the top, followed by AFS provisions that implement specific duty areas, and occasionally supplemented by EU regulations that apply directly. Employment-law statutes form a parallel track with their own internal hierarchy (LAS, MBL, various leave acts).

**Amendment frequency:** 24 of 27 SFS statutes carry amendment references, many updated as recently as 2024-2025. This signals a high-maintenance compliance area requiring regular review. The AFS provisions were substantially restructured effective January 2025 through the AFS 2023 consolidation.

**Compliance obligation density:** The HR section (21 documents) carries the highest obligation density for service companies. Each statute creates discrete employer duties: documentation, notification, reporting, and process requirements. A typical mid-sized consulting firm faces 50+ discrete compliance obligations from section 02 alone.

**Service-sector relevance calibration:** The list successfully removes industrial-physical provisions while retaining all psychosocial, organizational, and administrative safety requirements. The inclusion of display-screen work, ergonomics, and lone-work provisions reflects accurate targeting of service-company risk profiles.

## 5. Laglig.se Recommendations

1. **Priority mapping for service companies:** Sections 01 and 02 should be the primary onboarding focus -- they contain the obligations that trigger most regulatory inspections and labor-court disputes.

2. **SAM-OSA integration:** AFS 2023:1 (SAM) and AFS 2023:2 kap. 2 (OSA) should be presented together in any compliance workflow, as systematic safety management and psychosocial risk assessment are operationally inseparable for office-based employers.

3. **Display-screen compliance module:** The display-screen provisions (section 04, items 1-2) affect virtually 100% of service-company employees and warrant a dedicated self-assessment tool covering workstation setup, lighting, breaks, and eye examinations.

4. **Staffing-agency compliance path:** The combination of Lag (2012:854), LAS, and the posted-workers act creates a specialized compliance path for consulting and staffing firms that should be surfaced as a distinct workflow.

5. **Chemical safety -- minimal but mandatory:** While section 05 is small, REACH and CLP carry significant penalties. A simplified "office chemicals" checklist (cleaning products, hand sanitizer, toner) would serve this audience better than the full industrial chemical-management framework.

6. **Fire-safety integration:** Section 06 obligations (systematic fire-safety work, evacuation plans) overlap with the SAM framework in section 01 and should be linked in any compliance dashboard.

## 7. Agent Training Annotations

### Compliance Summary Examples (laglig.se originals)

**Example 1 -- SAM obligations (AFS 2023:1):**

> As an employer, you must operate a documented systematic work-environment management process. This includes maintaining a written work-environment policy, conducting regular risk assessments, investigating incidents, distributing safety responsibilities to line managers, and performing an annual management-system review. If internal expertise is insufficient, you must engage occupational health services.

**Example 2 -- Display-screen work (AFS 2023:11 kap. 3):**

> If your employees perform regular display-screen work, you must ensure workstations are ergonomically designed with adjustable furniture, adequate lighting, and glare-free screens. Employees are entitled to periodic eye examinations at the employer's expense, and corrective lenses must be provided if needed specifically for screen work. Work routines must include sufficient breaks or task variation to prevent repetitive strain.

**Example 3 -- Whistleblower channel (SFS 2021:890):**

> Employers with more than 50 employees must establish an internal reporting channel where workers can report serious misconduct of public interest. The channel must protect the reporter's identity, follow up on reports within a defined timeframe, and guard against retaliation. Smaller employers are encouraged but not required to establish formal channels.

**Example 4 -- Fire-safety management (SFS 2003:778 + SRVFS 2004:3):**

> Every employer operating from physical premises must conduct systematic fire-safety work. This includes documenting fire risks, maintaining evacuation plans, conducting regular fire drills, ensuring fire-safety installations (alarms, extinguishers, emergency lighting) are inspected and maintained, and designating responsible persons for fire-safety coordination.
