# Livsmedel Sverige -- Laglistaanalys

## 1. Overview

The Livsmedel Sverige list contains 53 documents covering the full breadth of Swedish and EU food-sector regulation. It is the most domain-specific list in the Notisum collection: only a single document (SKVFS 2015:6 on staff registers) overlaps with the Arbetsmiljo list, and one or two entries touch environmental/water topics shared with the Miljo lists. The remaining 50+ documents are unique to this food-focused collection.

All 53 documents currently sit in a single UNCATEGORIZED bucket with no internal structure. The list spans Swedish parliamentary statutes (SFS), Livsmedelsverket agency rules (LIVSFS), Jordbruksverket agency rules (SJVFS), Havs- och vattenmyndigheten rules (HVMFS), one Skatteverket rule (SKVFS), one older Livsmedelsverket rule under the former prefix (SLVFS), two industry-guidance documents (OVRM), one Livsmedelsverket vägledning (MXXA), and 11 directly-applicable EU/EC regulations.

**Key characteristics:**

- Heavily operational: most rules create day-to-day obligations for food handlers, manufacturers, and retailers.
- Dual-layer regulation: Swedish SFS/LIVSFS rules frequently implement or complement EU regulations that also appear independently in the list, creating a tight web of cross-references.
- Time span from 1993 (SLVFS 1993:20 on batch identification marking) to 2025 (SJVFS 2025:2 on fruit/vegetable quality controls and EU 40/2025 on packaging).
- Multiple amendment chains: many documents carry amendment SFS references showing active regulatory evolution.

**Proposed restructuring:** 7 original sections built around functional compliance themes rather than legal hierarchy, designed to help food-business operators quickly locate the rules that apply to their specific activities.

## 4. Content Pattern Assessment

### Thematic clusters

**Cluster 1 -- The "hygiene stack":** A tightly coupled group where EU regulations (EG) 852/2004 and (EG) 853/2004 set baseline and animal-origin hygiene requirements, LIVSFS 2005:20 adds Swedish facility registration and operational details, and LIVSFS 2006:12 addresses the cold chain. These four documents are functionally inseparable for any food business.

**Cluster 2 -- The "labeling stack":** (EU) 1169/2011 is the master labeling regulation, complemented by Swedish-language rules in LIVSFS 2014:4, batch-marking in SLVFS 1993:20, and protected-designation controls in SFS 2009:1424/1425. Any business selling packaged food encounters all of these simultaneously.

**Cluster 3 -- The "chemical safety stack":** Additives ((EG) 1333/2008), contaminant limits ((EU) 915/2023 and LIVSFS 2012:3), pesticide residues ((EU) 2609/2024), irradiation (LIVSFS 2000:46), and food-contact materials ((EG) 1935/2004, LIVSFS 2023:5, (EU) 3190/2024) together define what can and cannot be present in food and its packaging.

**Cluster 4 -- The "control infrastructure stack":** (EU) 625/2017 establishes the EU control framework, Swedish agencies layer on specific control duties (LIVSFS 2005:21, 2005:22), fee structures (LIVSFS 2021:8, SJVFS 2019:75), reporting duties (LIVSFS 2009:13), risk classification (MXXA7056), and import/export controls (SFS 2006:812, SFS 2011:1060).

### Cross-reference density

This list exhibits the highest internal cross-reference density of any Notisum list examined. Virtually every LIVSFS document references the core SFS 2006:804 and SFS 2006:813 pair, and many also reference the EU-level regulations they supplement. For laglig.se, this means compliance paths rarely involve a single document -- the agent must guide users through interconnected rule sets.

### Temporal patterns

- Oldest active rule: SLVFS 1993:20 (batch identification -- 32 years old, still in force)
- Newest entries: SJVFS 2025:2, (EU) 40/2025 (both from 2025)
- Significant recent activity: 6 documents from 2023-2025 showing continued regulatory evolution, especially in packaging, contaminant limits, and BPA restrictions

## 5. Laglig.se Recommendations

### 5.1 Prioritization for platform build-out

**Tier 1 -- Build first (highest user impact):**

- Section B (Hygiene) -- every food business must comply from day one of operations
- Section C (Labeling) -- affects every packaged food product sold in Sweden
- Section A (Foundational law) -- required context for understanding all other sections

**Tier 2 -- Build second:**

- Section D (Additives/contaminants) -- essential for manufacturers and importers
- Section F (Official controls) -- operators need to understand inspections and fee obligations

**Tier 3 -- Build third:**

- Section E (Food-contact materials) -- narrower audience (manufacturers, material suppliers)
- Section G (Trade practices/guidance) -- supplementary compliance context

### 5.2 Agent-behavior notes

- When a user asks about starting a restaurant, the agent should guide through Section A (registration basis in SFS 2006:804), Section B (hygiene and facility approval via LIVSFS 2005:20), Section F (risk classification via MXXA7056 and control fees via LIVSFS 2021:8), and Section G (SKVFS 2015:6 on staff registers and OVRM 51 for fast-food guidance).
- When a user asks about food labeling, the agent should present the EU baseline ((EU) 1169/2011) together with Swedish specifics (LIVSFS 2014:4), then check if protected designations or price-information rules also apply.
- For import/export questions, the agent should route to SFS 2006:812 (import) or SFS 2011:1060 (export) in Section F, combined with relevant hygiene rules from Section B.
- The agent should flag that OVRM documents (Section G) are non-binding industry guidance -- helpful but not substitutes for the legal requirements they interpret.

### 5.3 Content-gap observations

- **Missing:** No specific regulation on food fraud or authenticity testing (covered partly by general EU food law but not specifically listed).
- **Missing:** No explicit allergen-management regulation beyond what is embedded in (EU) 1169/2011 labeling rules.
- **Missing:** No specific food-waste reduction rules (the EU packaging regulation (EU) 40/2025 touches packaging waste, but food-waste targets from EU directive 2018/851 are absent).
- **Potentially outdated:** SLVFS 1993:20 on batch marking -- while still in force, its practical requirements are largely subsumed by EU traceability rules in (EG) 178/2002. Consider flagging this for users.

### 5.4 Overlap management

- **SKVFS 2015:6** (staff registers) also appears in the Arbetsmiljo list. The laglig.se agent should present this in whichever context the user is asking about (food operations vs. workplace safety) without duplication in compliance checklists.
- **SFS 2019:556** (water management) may overlap with Miljo lists. Water-supply rules have dual relevance for food businesses (production input) and environmental compliance (resource management).
- **SFS 2022:725** (plant protection) may overlap with agricultural-sector lists if those are developed later.
